
# Final Recommendation

## Executive Summary

A multiple linear regression analysis was conducted to identify the business factors most strongly associated with monthly sales across retail stores. The final regression model explained approximately **79.98% of the variation in monthly sales (R² = 0.7998)**, indicating that the selected predictors provide a strong explanation of sales performance.

The analysis found that **marketing spend, customer footfall, average discount percentage, and store type** are significantly associated with monthly sales. These findings can help leadership make informed decisions regarding marketing investments, pricing strategies, and store operations.

---

# Which Factors Appear Most Strongly Associated with Monthly Sales?

The regression analysis identified the following variables as having the strongest association with monthly sales:

### 1. Customer Footfall

Customer footfall had a strong positive relationship with monthly sales (Coefficient = **29.11**, p < 0.05). Stores with higher customer traffic consistently achieved higher sales, making footfall one of the strongest drivers of store performance.

### 2. Marketing Spend

Marketing spend was positively associated with monthly sales (Coefficient = **1.115**, p < 0.05). This suggests that increased investment in marketing activities is associated with higher sales.

### 3. Store Type

Store type also had a significant influence on sales performance.

Compared with the reference store type (Residential):

* **Airport stores** generated approximately **35,614** additional sales units.
* **Mall stores** generated approximately **26,173** additional sales units.
* **High Street stores** generated approximately **17,099** additional sales units.

This indicates that store format and location contribute meaningfully to sales differences.

### 4. Average Discount Percentage

Average discount percentage showed a statistically significant **negative** relationship with monthly sales (Coefficient = **-82,179.33**, p = 0.035). This suggests that higher discount levels are associated with lower monthly sales after accounting for the other variables in the model.

---

# Which Variables Should Leadership Focus On?

Based on the regression results, leadership should prioritize the following areas:

* **Marketing Investment:** Continue investing in targeted marketing campaigns that attract customers and support sales growth.
* **Customer Footfall:** Implement initiatives such as loyalty programs, local promotions, and improved in-store experiences to increase customer visits.
* **Store Strategy:** Evaluate expansion and investment opportunities for higher-performing store types, particularly Airport and Mall stores.
* **Discount Strategy:** Review current discount policies to ensure discounts are applied strategically rather than routinely, balancing customer attraction with profitability.

---

# Which Variables Should Not Be Over-Interpreted?

**Staff Count** should be interpreted cautiously.

Although the coefficient is positive, its **p-value (0.069)** is greater than the commonly used significance level of 0.05. This means there is insufficient statistical evidence to conclude that increasing staff alone will lead to higher monthly sales after accounting for the other variables in the model.

Leadership should therefore avoid making staffing decisions based solely on this regression analysis.

---

# Recommended Business Actions

Based on the findings, the following actions are recommended:

1. Increase investment in effective marketing activities that generate customer traffic.
2. Focus on initiatives that improve customer footfall, as customer visits are one of the strongest drivers of sales.
3. Monitor and optimise discount strategies to avoid excessive discounting that may reduce overall sales revenue.
4. Continue investing in high-performing store formats and identify successful operational practices that can be applied across other store types.
5. Regularly update the regression model with new data to support ongoing business planning and performance monitoring.

---

# Risks and Limitations

The results should be interpreted with the following limitations in mind:

* The model assumes a linear relationship between the selected variables and monthly sales.
* Approximately **20% of the variation in monthly sales** remains unexplained, indicating that other factors—such as local competition, economic conditions, seasonality, weather, product assortment, and customer demographics—may also influence sales.
* The analysis is based on historical data and may not fully predict future market conditions.
* Regression results depend on the quality and completeness of the available data.

---

# Why Regression Shows Association but Not Causation

Regression analysis identifies statistical relationships between variables, but it does not prove that one variable directly causes another.

For example, the model shows that higher marketing spend is associated with higher monthly sales. However, this does not necessarily mean that increasing marketing expenditure alone will always increase sales. Other factors, such as seasonal demand, promotional campaigns, or local market conditions, may also contribute to the observed relationship.

Similarly, the negative relationship between average discount percentage and monthly sales does not prove that discounts reduce sales. It may reflect that stores experiencing weaker sales tend to offer larger discounts.

Establishing a cause-and-effect relationship would require additional evidence, such as controlled experiments or A/B testing.

---

# Final Recommendation

The **multiple regression model** should be adopted as the primary model for supporting business decisions because it explains approximately **80% of the variation in monthly sales** and identifies the key factors associated with store performance.

Leadership should focus on increasing customer footfall, optimising marketing investment, improving the performance of high-performing store formats, and implementing a disciplined discount strategy. At the same time, management should recognise that regression analysis identifies associations rather than definitive cause-and-effect relationships, and business decisions should be supported by additional operational insights and ongoing performance monitoring.
